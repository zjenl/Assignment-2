Global Happiness Index 2023: What Makes People Happy?

How happy are people today? How satisfied with their lives are people in different societies? And how do our living conditions affect all of this?

Digging into these big questions is more than just a curious endeavorâ€”it's deeply personal. In fact, happiness has become a hot topic everywhere, from coffee shop chats to serious economic research.

The World Happiness Report of 2023 endeavors to shed light on these very questions. The study is conducted by surveying representative samples from diverse nations, with individuals rating their lives on varied parameters and taking the average of these responses.

From the map above, we can see distinct patterns emerge when looking at continental trends. Oceania, particularly Australia, stands out with the highest happiness index, recording a value of 7.095. This is closely followed by North America, which secures the second position with the index ranging from 6-7. Interestingly, South America exhibits happiness levels akin to Europe, both continents predominantly falling within the 5-7 range. Asia, while vast and diverse, predominantly displays happiness values between 5 and 6. Africa registers the lowest values, with most countries on the continent showing happiness indices mainly in the 3-5 range.

What leads to such diversity? We are hoping to get a clearer picture of what's driving happiness (or maybe the lack of it) around the globe by zooming in on five key areas: economy, environment, health, community, and safety. By delving into these domains, we aspire to paint a holistic picture of global happiness in 2023, providing insights and unraveling the factors that sculpt the landscape of global contentment.

Economy

All currency are in USD.

From the scatter plot, it's evident that there's a positive correlation between a country's GDP per capita and its happiness index. Countries with a GDP per capita in the range of $50k to $100k often exhibit a higher happiness index. Conversely, lower unemployment rates, particularly those below 0.1%, are also indicative of higher happiness scores, suggesting that job security and economic stability are significant contributors to overall well-being. Boasting the highest happiness index globally, Australia has a GDP per capita of $54k and an unemployment rate of 5.36%. Such data reinforces the notion that while happiness is multifaceted and influenced by various factors, a robust economy plays a crucial role in enhancing the quality of life and overall satisfaction of its inhabitants.

Trends across Genders

One interesting trend is that bullying rates have decreased across the years in all regions and genders. Of note is that the bullying rate in boys is now 1.9% lower than that in girls, despite starting 2.2% higher. This change occurred between 2010 and 2011. We speculate that this may be due to the popularisation of social media around this time, allowing for different avenues of bullying, such as cyberbullying, to emerge.

Melbourne Metro Region

The Melbourne metropolitan region has generally reported lower bullying rates than across the state, despite having a range of NAPLAN scores (see above). This may be due to a variety of factors as any factor that attracts families to the city could be an explanation. Some possible examples are the parents education and profession, the funding of the school in question, or even the immigration status of students.

Within metropolitan Melbourne, KNOX reported the most bullying in 2016 at 22.6%, and Stonnington the least at 6%.

Decline in Bullying

Finally, inspecting the nine most populous LGAs, we find that there has been substantial improvement across the years. Across all nine LGAs, less than one in five students report having experienced bullying recently, a significant improvement.

Melbourne, in particular, has always had low bullying numbers, but has since been joined by Monash; both at one student in every eight.
