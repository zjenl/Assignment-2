Global Happiness Index 2023: What Makes People Happy?

How happy are people today? How satisfied with their lives are people in different societies? And how do our living conditions affect all of this?

Digging into these big questions is more than just a curious endeavorâ€”it's deeply personal. In fact, happiness has become a hot topic everywhere, from coffee shop chats to serious economic research.

The World Happiness Report of 2023 endeavors to shed light on these very questions. The study is conducted by surveying representative samples from diverse nations, with individuals rating their lives on varied parameters and taking the average of these responses.

From the map above, we can see distinct patterns emerge when looking at continental trends. Oceania, particularly Australia, stands out with the highest happiness index, recording a value of 7.095. This is closely followed by North America, which secures the second position with the index ranging from 6-. Interestingly, South America exhibits happiness levels akin to

Europe, both continents predominantly falling within the 5-7 range. Asia, while vast and diverse, predominantly displays happiness values between 5 and 6. Africa registers the lowest values, with most countries on the continent showing happiness indices mainly in the 3-5 range.

What leads to such diversity? We are hoping

to get a clearer picture of what's driving happiness (or maybe the lack of it) around the globe by zooming in on five key areas: economy, environment, health, community, and safety. By delving into these domains, we aspire to paint a holistic picture of global happiness in 2023, providing insights and unraveling the factors that sculpt the landscape of global contentment.

Economy

In the scatter plot above, each point represents a country. Hover over to view details.

From the scatter plot, it's evident that there's a positive correlation between a country's GDP per capita and its happiness index. Countries with a GDP per capita in the range of $50k to $100k often exhibit a higher happiness index. Conversely, lower unemployment rates, particularly those below 0.1%, are also indicative of higher happiness scores, suggesting that job security and economic stability are significant contributors to overall well-being.

Boasting the highest happiness index globally, Australia has a GDP per capita of $54k and an unemployment rate of 5.36%. Such data reinforces the notion that while happiness is multifaceted and influenced by various factors, a robust economy plays a crucial role in enhancing the quality of life and overall satisfaction of its inhabitants.

CPI (Consumer Price Index) also plays an important role in people's happiness. It is a measure that assesses the average change in prices paid by consumers for goods and services over time. It is vital because it helps determine the purchasing power of money, indicating inflation trends and guiding economic policies. The inflation rate gives information about how the country's CPI has changed from 2022 to 2023, with the negative numbers indicating a drop.

From the parallel plot, we can see that most countries have lower inflation rates (below 2.5%), and the Consumer Price Index generally exhibits an inverse relationship with the happiness index. Such trends reflect tendency toward price stability and that elevated living costs decreases levels of overall happiness in a country.

Environment

This heatmap displays the relationship between a country's population density (X-axis) and urban population percentage (Y-axis). The color intensity indicates the number of countries in each segment, while the circles signify their average happiness score. Use the histograms above and to the right for distribution based on density and urbanisation rate, respectively.

Environment significantly influences people's well-being, with factors like population density and urbanisation rate playing key roles. Density refers to the number of people per km squared, and urbanisation rate refers to the percentage of the country's population living in urban areas. Densely populated areas can lead to challenges like overcrowding and noise pollution, reducing people's overall quality of life. Meanwhile, higher urbanisation rate offers better job opportunities and infrastructure, though they also introduce issues such as pollution and high living costs.

In the heatmap, we can see that most countries have a density between 0-150 people/kmsq, while the urbanisation rate is more distributed. Eventhough there isn't a strict linear relationship, we can see that low density and high urbanisation rate are often correlated with higher happiness index of the country.

Health

Health is fundamental to happiness as it enables individuals to live fulfilling lives, pursue their goals, and enjoy daily activities without physical or mental hindrance. Understanding the main causes of death and average life expectancies provides insights into public health challenges, helping societies prioritise interventions and allocate resources efficiently to enhance the well-being and longevity of their populations.

From the donut chart, we can see that cardiovascular diseases and neoplasms accounts for most of the deaths in the world. These 2 diseases are the most severe in Europe and Oceania, however in Africa, respiratory infections and tuberculosis replaces neoplasms to take the second rank. We can also see a clear positive correlation between life expectancies and happiness index, with Oceania having the highest life expectancy of 82.30 years and Africa having the lowest life expectancy of 64.31 years.

The isotype dot plot below shows the average number of medical doctors available per 10,000 population in different continents ranked in descending order of the happiness index. In general, higher density of medical doctors is correlated with higher happiness indexes, as it improves the overall health of the country.

The donut chart illustrates the major causes of death in 2023, with each segment representing a different cause. The central number showcases the average life expectancy across the selected data. Below, the bar chart provides a distribution of happiness scores, which you can use to filter and explore the relationship between happiness and causes of death.

In this graph, each icon represents a doctor. More icons indicate a higher doctor density in that continent.

Community

This graph plots the relationship between a country's social support and its citizens' freedom to make life choices.
The color intensity indicates the number of countries in each segment, while the circles signify their average happiness score. Use the sidebars to see the distribution of countries based on social support and freedom.

Community plays a pivotal role in the happiness index, serving as a foundation for emotional and social well-being. Sense of social support, defined by the World's Happiness Report, is the availability of help and care from family, friends, or communities. Freedom is referred as the ability to make life choices without undue constraints. It also includes Human Rights, including the right to life and liberty, freedom from slavery and torture, freedom of opinion and expression, the right to work and education, and many more. These two measures are in the format of a score out of one, and they directly influence individuals' perceptions of happiness.

From the heatmap, it seems that countries with higher levels of social support and greater freedom to make life choices generally score higher on the happiness index. Additionally, the majority of countries are clustered around higher social support values and moderate freedom levels, indicating these factors might be prevalent across many nations. On the other hand, countries with low social support and freedom scores are very likely to have very low happiness scores.

Safety

Safety plays a pivotal role in the happiness index. The criminality score by Global Organised Crime Index, a measure of the prevalence and severity of crimes in a region, can significantly affect happiness; areas with higher criminality often report lower happiness levels due to increased fear and insecurity. Additionally, the size of the armed forces can influence perceptions of safety; a well-sized and equipped military can deter external threats, further enhancing a nation's sense of security and, in turn, its collective happiness.

From the scatter plot, we can see that in general, lower criminality aligns with greater national happiness, and this two attributes exhibits a negative correlation. Armed forced size on the other hand, does not directly correlate with happiness or crime. Countries like the USA and Australia with moderate sized armed forces boast higher happiness levels, while Afghanistan presents low happiness with high criminality with similar sized armed force. Countries with vast populations like China and India also have large armed forces, though this doesn't directly correlate with happiness or crime.
